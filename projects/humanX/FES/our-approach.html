To further ecological, social and economic well-being, FES:

  1. 1.

    Promotes the decentralisation of natural resource management by aiding informed, local-level, collective action.

  2. 2.

    Demonstrates the importance of conservation by focusing on Commons within the larger landscape.

  3. 3.

    Assists local communities to secure rights over shared natural resources, improve local governance and access public funds to restore local ecosystems.

To address the critical task of Commons restoration, FES centre-stages local communities as stewards capable of leading local action, and promotes working on Commons within the larger landscape.

This enhances local employment, livestock production, farm productivity and the overall returns that are within the ecological thresholds of the area.

By working across varied ecological and cultural settings, FES contributes to the widespread recognition of Common Property Regimes as a promising option, alongside centralised or individual property regimes. Instead of a nature or people-centric approach, FES reinforces socio-ecological thinking for improved governance of resource systems and local stewardship.

Forests contribute positively to key ecological functions:

Improved transfer of nutrients, longer periods of water flow, moisture retention, and better pollination and pest control. They also extend resilience to farming systems, impact local livelihoods, and influence critical water supplies to our cities.

FES works with forest-dependent communities to improve ecological functions and services, the backbone of agriculture and livestock-dependent communities. We assist them in creating context-based action plans that conserve local ecosystems, augment incomes and enhance local decision-making.

Ours is a landscape approach, which creates opportunities to manage different geographies for preservation, conservation and extraction.

It also highlights the importance of a systems view and the organic links between forests, pastures and other production systems in the landscape. In this way, FES helps build a larger farmer constituency for forest conservation.

Infographic

Rather than associating farming with crop production, FES recognises the value of the interconnections within the larger farming system, which includes natural resources beyond the farm.

FES engages with local communities to address subsistence requirements, generate incomes, and influence choices that impact ecological thresholds over a period of time.

A systems understanding is innate and latent within rural communities; efforts to build interlinkages between agriculture, livestock, and pastures or forests find ready acceptance. Community action for the restoration of Commons often triggers collective decision-making on crop choices, by considering groundwater as common property, and nurturing pollinator and pest predator habitats to improve crop productivity.

The recognition of the role of Panchayats and Gram Sabhas (in Scheduled Areas) for the

governance of shared natural resources, and the impetus on decentralised governance through the 73rd amendment of the Constitution, provide strength to the democratic character of the Gram Sabhas and Panchayats, the foundation of our governance architecture.

Understanding the importance of strengthening this first-mile governance, FES helps rural communities enhance collective action, democratise the functioning of local institutions, and devise institutional spaces that safeguard the interests of the poor.

We seek to bolster active, region-specific stewardship.

This, in turn, strengthens collaborative platforms where community institutions, government functionaries, academics and NGOs deliberate and decide on conservation and the use of natural resources.

Infographic

The degradation of Commons can be attributed to weak or absent tenure rights, erosion of local institutions, and an unsubstantiated but generic view that local communities are ineffective managers of shared natural resources.

FES assists rural communities gain tenure rights over their Commons, within the available administrative arrangements and legal provisions:

  1. 1.

    Where the Forest Rights Act is applicable, we support tribal communities’ claims on these areas as community forests.

  2. 2.

    Where there are ‘wastelands’, we help village communities acquire long-term leases, and also claim for increases in areas designated pastures, backed by evidenced increase in livestock.

  3. 3.

    We also enable local communities to map and register their common lands in the Prohibitory Order Book (POB) or Panchayat Asset Register to prevent their diversion for other uses.

Infographic

Institutional investments such as long-term tenure rights for communities and strong village-level institutions, are crucial to address failing soil health, depleting groundwater levels, and loss of biodiversity.

To build and sustain community assets, FES supports Panchayats to align Commons’ restoration plans with the Right to Employment Act. We assist in establishing democratic and transparent decision-making systems that enable habitation-level institutions to implement Commons programmes effectively.

FES collaborates with various levels of government to conduct awareness campaigns on Commons restoration through public funds.

In response to these campaigns, we conduct capacity-building programmes so that communities can self-organise to avail these funds for ecological restoration.
