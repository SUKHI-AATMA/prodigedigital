The data speaks volumes, and it speaks clearly: Our natural resources are finite. Without careful tending, the downward spiral of loss quickens, of the Earth we share, of resources, livelihoods, and economies.

The data also underscores this fact: it is impossible to protect one without nurturing the other. Depleting natural resources degrades the quality of all life, and it is these larger, inter-dependent ecosystems that we need to fight for.

It is our collective and individual responsibility to preserve and tend to the world we inhabit. We need to deliver on the Promise of Commons, to promote ecological, social and economic well-being.

Overworked pastures. Shrinking forests…

The degradation of natural resources that belong to and affect whole communities is plain to see. Almost two decades ago, these shared resources were estimated at a value of USD 5 billion annually. The social and economic fallout is felt most acutely by India’s rural economies, whose livelihoods are directly linked to their environment.

Greater still, these Commons regulate water and nutrient flows, sequester carbon, contribute to pollination and pest control, serving as buffers to ecologically fragile areas and as repositories of biodiversity.

This is a cycle of loss that we must actively break out of.

FES pursues ecological security with rural communities at multiple levels, linking traditional and modern knowledge systems and methods that place these communities at the centre of every decision-making process.

The 73rd Amendment to the Constitution and the Forests Rights Act both recognise that local self-governance enables collective action, and better management of natural resources.

Giving rural communities charge of their futures is the quickest, most sustainable way to enable revival, because ownership at this level transcends administrative borders, and transforms into stewardship.

FES works towards a rights-based management system that fosters community ownership. We seek equitable environmental governance through collaboration, with the government and with local communities.

The destruction of our natural ecosystems has a ripple effect, immediately felt by the rural economies that depend on them.

Falling yields and increased cost of cultivation are just some of the challenges faced by millions across India. Annually, the Indian government spends INR 60 thousand crores on activities under natural resource management.

We believe that resilience can be found through systemic sustainability;

through the better management of our Commons and farming methods, by strengthening farming and non-farming livelihoods with improved food and livestock production systems, reduced expenditure, and multiple sources of income generation.
